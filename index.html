IVF

In vitro fertilization (IVF) is a type of assisted reproductive technology (ART) where a male sperm and a female egg are fertilized outside of the human body. This option is typically used my couples experiencing fertility issues. There are seven steps to IVF listed below, and an attached video for you to understand them.

Fertility Testing

Fertility testing evaluates reproductive health in both women and men to identify potential issues affecting the ability to conceive. It's general recommended if you are under 35 years of age and have not conceived after one year of unprotected sex (sex without any type of birth control), or you are over 35 years of age and have not conceived after six months of unprotected sex, or you have irregular cycles. (cycles that are more than 35 days apart)

Egg and Sperm donation

Egg and sperm donation involves the donation of reproductive cells to assist individuals or couples facing infertility. Sperm that were donatede are often used in cases of male infertility, or when there is a risk of passing on genetic conditions. Eggs that were donated a used in female infertility cases, allowing the intended parents to have a genetic link to their child.

Meet the staff roles!

Endocrinologist

A reproductive endocrinologist is the only type of medical doctor with specialized training focused solely on helping people become and stay pregnant.

Fertility Nurse

Fertility nurses are registered nurses with specialized training in reproductive medicine.

Embryologist

‍Embryologists are scientists who specialize in the study and manipulation of embryos. In the fertility clinic, they are responsible for handling eggs, sperm, and embryos in the laboratory.

Andrologist

Andrologists focus on male fertility and reproductive health. They conduct semen analyses, assess sperm quality, and perform procedures such as sperm retrieval for assisted reproductive techniques.

‍

Genetic Counselor

Genetic counselors provide valuable support and information to patients undergoing fertility treatment. They help assess the risk of genetic disorders, explain genetic testing options, and interpret test results.
